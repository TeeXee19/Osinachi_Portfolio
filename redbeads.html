“So you’re saying that Uzor prefers ‘Sophie’to you?” Kaima asked me, her eyebrows furrowed in that way it usually is when something she doesn’t understand happens, like when she was made ‘Employee of the month’ last May.

“Well, at least he was sincere about the breakup. He said I’m too… earthy,” If I could furrow my brows too, at this point I would have.

>

“Earthy?! What does that even mean?”

“Kaima, if I knew, I would’ve told you. But I don’t… so please stop asking me questions so I
can take the elevator up to our apartment with some level of dignity? I can’t cry here, my
eyeliner isn’t waterproof and I know carbon is good for the skin but…” Who was I even
kidding? I was already crying into her neck. I didn’t care if we were on an elevator and
someone could come in any second. Wouldn’t be the worst thing that happened to me today
anyway.
Kaima patted my backas she consoled me. “Hey, Bubu, stop crying. If Uzor didn’t know what
he had in you, well…he’s more hopeless than a purewater hawker on Third Mainland Bridge,”
I couldn’t help it. I just had to laugh.
“What on earth is a purewater hawker doing on Third Mainland Bridge?”
“My point exactly,” she said with a smile and gave me her handkerchief to dry my tears just
as the elevator door opened on our floor and we stepped out into the hallway. As we walked
down to the end of the corridor where our apartment was, I couldn’t help feeling blessed to
have a bestfriend like her. Chikaima and I had known ourselves since junior secondary
school when she was always getting bullied for being a rich kid in a middle class girl’s
boarding school in Enugu. What she had been doing there in the first place was still a
mystery to me. We clicked instantly because of our writing skills and shared interests in
books. Then coincidentally, both our families moved to Lagos so we never stopped seeing
each other. We didn’t go to the same school again however, probably because her father
earned a six to seven figures salary and mine earned exactly five figures. Kaima always said
it was because they both had different views towards education. While I went to a standard
senior secondary school and university to ‘learn,pass and get a job to feed myself’, Kaima
first went to an arts school and then to an actual writing college. I didn’t even know those
existed in Nigeria. I graduated with First class honours in Law but all I really wanted to do
was write. My dad didn’t want to listen but Kaima did. She sent my CV and written works to
the same magazine she was applying for in New York, we both got jobs there and now...
well, my brother wants to be a musician and Dad is helping him buy instruments. All in all,
life was moving perfectly. Until Uzor...
“Hey, earthy! The fact that there are no mosquitoes in America is no reason to leave the
door wide open like that. Come inside joor.” Stepping into our apartment was like going
backhome. From the portrait of Bright Chimezie above our flat screen TV, to the red Isiagu
material we cut and somehow sew into pillow covers for our monochrome chair set. I
always felt that touch was extra but there’s no stopping Kaima. I sat down heavily on the
couch and took off my stilettos.
“What are you having? There’s ofe oha in the fridge. But I can make yam and red oil for you. I
know that’s your comfort food,” Kaima had already changed to her casual clothes and was
leaning on the door leading from her room into the living room.
“I don’t want to eat.”
“Hmm, what about nkwobi? It would be hard to get and pretty expensive too but if it’s gonna
cheer you up then―”
“What do you think he meant by earthy? Is it the hair? Should I relax it? Or invest in
weave-ons or something?”
“No, no, no. Come on, Ebube. You know better than this.”
“But if he doesn’t like the way I am then how sure am I that the next guy will? I’m 23 already
and you know what happens when an Igbo woman turns 25 without getting married. I really
don’t want to go back home for an inquisition,” I felt the tears coming up again. Kaima
crossed the room and sat on the couch beside me.
“Did you just say ‘...if he doesn’t like the way you are...’? What ‘way’ are you exactly?
“I dunno...traditional?”
“Wow. So... you prefer your hair natural and you prefer highlife to hip hop? I doubt if that’s a
terrible thing. That’s how you were raised. It’s who you are. If he doesn’t like it, then he
should get lost.”
“But, he’s Igbo too. And he doesn’t go all out like we do―” I pointed at the centre table “―
look. Even our side stools and centre table are replicas of the ones you saw in a chief’s obi
in Enugu,”
Kaima rolled her eyes at me, “Yes, I know our apartment looks like a mini Igbo-ukwu shrine.
But you love it just as much as I do. Uzor may be Igbo, but he’s not as connected to the
culture as we are. That’s literally the only thing both our parents agree on. Probably the only
reason why they let us keep being friends in the first place.”
“Well, that and the fact that your mum makes the best achicha my father has ever tasted,”
Kaima and I burst out laughing. When we were done, she stood up from the couch and
turned to me,
“Bubu, thing is, if he doesn’t appreciate our culture, he doesn’t appreciate you. And if he
doesn’t appreciate you, he’s really not meant for you. Besides, what would your dad say when
he finds out you changed yourself for some alleged Igbo dude that can’t speak Igbo and
hates ofe onugbu?” She paused for effect. I nearly rolled my eyes. Kaima was such a
drama queen.
“Biko, I’ve heard you. Can you go and microwave the soup now? Agụụ na-agụ m,”
“I’ve lost energy to cook. I’m going to order pizza,” and she began to walk back to her room. I
stared in shock for a few seconds then quickly grabbed her hand.
“So after all this talk about culture and being yourself and all what not, you’re going to order
pizza?”
“Nwanne, nke ahu gbasara gi o. Ike agwula m.”
“You know what? I’ll do it myself. Need to keep my hands busy anyway.”
As I walked to the kitchen, I made a promise to myself. Maybe dancing to OsondiOwendi
instead of Bodak Yellow made me seem weird. Maybe wearing red beads on my wrist made
me look like a lost diviner in New York. But that’s what I was raised to appreciate and love. I
wouldn’t toss my culture aside for anything or anyone...
